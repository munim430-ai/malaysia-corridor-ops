## 1. Executive Summary

The Malaysia-Bangladesh labour corridor has been closed to new recruitment since June 1, 2024. It is reopening — Bangladesh's PM visited Kuala Lumpur in June 2026 to request it, both governments agreed to an Employer Pays Principle in April 2026, and Malaysia centralized quota processing into FWCMS eQuota in July 2026. No labour MoU is signed yet; best case is Q3-Q4 2026 for the MoU, Q1 2027 for a first new worker batch.

**This venture is a Bangladesh-side compliance and processing partner** — not a middleman, not a matchmaker — built to handle the functions no Malaysia-based platform can perform from Kuala Lumpur: BMET registration, medical, PDOT training, village sourcing, document collection, financing coordination, arrival handling, and bank guarantees.

**Revenue is 100% employer-funded.** The prior worker-pays pricing track is retired: it is not viable at any price a target worker's family can actually afford (§2.2). Worker financing (BRAC/ASA/Grameen) survives only as a bridge mechanism, never a pricing pillar.

**The plan's single gating dependency:** two regulatory unknowns — the TURAP fee structure and the BMET processing circular — must be resolved before any cost projection, timeline, or margin figure in this plan can be treated as more than a scenario. The most probable path to resolving them runs through an umbrella Recruiting Licence (RL) partnership, not independent outreach to ministries (§2.3-2.4).

**What's already built, at zero cost:** a bilingual marketing site, a 505-company employer database with sector/compliance scoring, outreach automation, and a 6-channel distribution playbook. **What three rounds of hardening added:** a redesigned, platform-agnostic MVP (§8); a defined pilot (§9); a data protection program (§10) and a restored payment/remittance compliance plan (§11); a revenue model reconciled against real costs (§6.4-6.5); a primary-validation action to replace secondary-sourced assumptions with real conversations (§4.3); and an honest team-capacity and Malaysia-presence plan (§12-13).

## 2. Market Situation and the Regulatory Gating Task

### 2.1 Facts as of 2026-07-19

| Fact | Status |
|---|---|
| Corridor closed for new BD recruitment | Since 2024-06-01 |
| Labour MoU | **Not signed** — only a cultural MoU (2026-06-22) |
| Employer Pays Principle | Bilaterally agreed, 2026-04-09 |
| FWCMS eQuota centralization | 2026-07-06 — case-by-case approvals abolished |
| FWCMS fee | RM215/worker (confirmed) |
| TURAP fee structure | **Undocumented, not Cabinet-confirmed** |
| BMET processing circular | **Not committed** |
| Malaysia's "10 conditions" for agencies | Would qualify only 5-7 of 102+ incumbent agencies |
| Sarawak FWTA fee | RM1,854/worker, under review after industry pushback |
| NIISe (replaces FWCMS/ePPAx) | Rolling out in 3 phases, March 2026-Oct 2028; **29.9% complete as of June 2026, slightly behind schedule** |
| KESUMA's own stakeholder engagement | Confirmed running through **source-country governments and the existing FWCMS technology provider (Bestinet)** — not open engagement with unaffiliated private ventures |

### 2.2 Why worker-pays is retired, in numbers

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Bangladesh legal maximum migration cost | Tk 78,990 |
| Actual costs paid in the current (exploitative) market | Tk 450,000-500,000 |
| Average migrant family remittance capacity | ~Tk 82,000 |

The *legal ceiling* on migration cost already exceeds average remittance capacity. There is no worker-facing price — not 2.5 lakh, not 3.0-3.5 lakh, not any number this venture previously considered — that is simultaneously affordable to the target family and commercially sustainable given the cost floor (§6.1). **Conclusion: don't compete on worker price. Compete on being the only channel that doesn't charge the worker at all.**

### 2.3 The regulatory gating task

Every cost projection, timeline, and margin figure in this plan is conditional on two unknowns: the TURAP fee (if TURAP applies at all) and the BMET processing circular. Until both are resolved, this plan's numbers are scenarios, not forecasts — and that should be said explicitly to every employer, partner, and RL holder this venture talks to. Overstating certainty here is a worse credibility risk than the uncertainty itself.

### 2.4 Corrected sequencing: pursue RL partnership first, not ministry outreach in parallel

Two facts, both now confirmed, change how this gating task should actually be pursued:
1. Malaysia's 10-condition regime and Bangladesh's entrenched incumbent-agency network make it unlikely that KESUMA/BMET grant meaningful access to an unlicensed, pre-revenue outside party.
2. KESUMA's own described stakeholder-engagement process runs through **source-country governments and the existing FWCMS operator** — not through open channels to unaffiliated private ventures.

**Sequencing, revised:** treat umbrella RL partnership (§3.3) as the lead workstream and the primary route to regulatory credibility — not a parallel track, and not equally weighted against a hopeful "independent ministry outreach" attempt. Independent outreach can still be attempted opportunistically (a written information request costs little), but it should not be budgeted as a real fallback path, and no timeline in this plan should assume it succeeds.

## 3. Business Model

### 3.1 What we are

A Bangladesh-side compliance and processing partner, positioned to Malaysian employers as a FWCMS/NIISe/TURAP-compatible fulfilment arm, funded entirely by them. To Bangladeshi workers: the zero-fee, transparent, direct channel — not "the cheaper channel," since at any price we'd charge workers directly, we are not actually affordable to them (§2.2).

### 3.2 The eight functions only a BD-side partner can perform

BMET registration & smart card; pre-departure medical; PDOT training; village-level worker sourcing; document collection; worker financing/microfinance coordination (bridge mechanism only); arrival handling; bank guarantee processing.

### 3.3 Legal structure and the RL partnership as the critical path

1. **Now:** information & registration service only — no recruiting, no money collection.
2. **Next, and now the lead priority (§2.4):** umbrella agreement with an existing BMET Recruiting Licence (RL) holder — 30-50% revenue share, evaluated explicitly on how much ministry credibility and access the RL holder brings, not only on revenue-share terms.
3. **Medium-term:** Malaysia entity (SSM company + KDN agency registration) + Bangladesh sourcing arm.
4. **At scale:** own BMET RL.

### 3.4 Pricing — 100% employer-funded

No worker-pays pricing track exists. Revenue is a per-worker or flat processing/compliance fee charged to the employer, sized against the cost scenarios in §6.3. Worker financing exists only as a bridge in specific batches where an employer-pays contract isn't yet finalized — never as this venture's own pricing, and never for more than the legal-maximum Tk 78,990, never the inflated market rate.

### 3.5 Differentiation

Not price — incumbents can and will temporarily undercut it. This venture's moat: real written regulatory confirmation obtained through genuine relationships (§2.4, §3.3) that competitors haven't bothered to secure; a full BMET/FOMEMA/FWCMS documentation chain; zero worker-paid fees structurally, not as marketing; and a working MVP (§8) that demonstrates compliance capability before a single worker moves.

This costs almost nothing and is the single best available test of whether the entire targeting thesis holds before more time goes into RL negotiation or MVP build.

## 8. The MVP — platform-agnostic by design

A dashboard tightly integrated with FWCMS was the original concept and is retired: no confirmed third-party API exists, and FWCMS itself is being replaced by NIISe (three-phase rollout through October 2028, 29.9% complete as of June 2026) — building against it now risks obsolescence before validation completes.

**The MVP instead: a guided compliance-readiness checklist and document tool**, independent of any government platform's API:
- Walks an employer through exactly what a Section 60K/eQuota application requires — quota justification, worker document checklists, levy/insurance proof — with status tracked in our own system.
- Value to the employer: clarity through a genuinely confusing bureaucratic process, regardless of which government system (FWCMS today, NIISe tomorrow, TURAP if adopted) is authoritative.
- If §2.3/§2.4 later produces confirmed third-party integration terms for any of these systems, integration becomes an enhancement layered on top — never a dependency the MVP's core value rests on.

Smaller and less impressive-sounding than a live-integrated dashboard. That's intentional: buildable and durable beats impressive and fragile.

## 10. Data Protection & PDPA Compliance Program

Malaysia's 2024 PDPA amendments extend the Security Principle directly to data processors — this venture takes on independent legal liability the moment it handles employer FWCMS-application data or worker documents (passport, medical, police clearance) via the MVP (§8) or the outreach CRM. Before either goes further:
- Name one accountable person for data protection (even solo-founder, initially).
- Minimum retention: collect only what the compliance-readiness checklist actually needs; don't retain full sensitive-document copies longer than the active transaction requires.
- Written data processing agreement with every employer client, even at pilot scale.
- Written breach/incident response protocol before the MVP's first real user.

---

## 11. Payment and Remittance Compliance (restored — dropped in an earlier draft, a defect this final version corrects)

Any money flow this venture touches — employer fee collection, worker financing disbursement, anything resembling escrow — is a regulated activity in Bangladesh. Bangladesh Bank's Foreign Exchange Policy Department (FEPD) circulars govern outward remittances and require transactions through Authorized Dealers (ADs). Before handling a single payment:
- Determine whether the chosen legal structure (§3.3) requires its own AD relationship or can route through a partner bank/RL holder's existing arrangement.
- Never hold worker or employer funds in an informal account structure.
- Resolve this alongside §2.3/§2.4, not as an afterthought once revenue starts.

## 12. Team & Capacity Plan

This plan now asks for several simultaneous demanding workstreams: RL/ministry relationship-building (§2.4/§3.3), primary validation (§4.3), MVP build (§8), pilot management (§9), and ongoing outreach (§5).

- **If solo-founder today:** sequence, don't parallelize. RL relationship-building should be the near-exclusive focus of the founder's time for the first 60-90 days. The MVP is scoped small enough (§8) to build in short, disciplined bursts or contract out.
- **Highest-leverage addition, if any hiring/partnership capacity exists:** not a developer — someone with existing Bangladesh recruitment-industry or Malaysia HR/compliance-consulting relationships, since that shortens §2.4/§3.3, the plan's actual critical path.
- **Discipline rule:** don't let outreach volume (§5) outrun the founder's actual capacity to have real regulatory/RL conversations.

## 13. Malaysia Presence — an open decision this plan flags rather than assumes away

Every existing tool and channel in this venture is Bangladesh-centric: a Bengali-first site, Bangladesh-registered WhatsApp numbers, a Dhaka-oriented outreach operation. But real RL and ministry relationship-building plausibly requires physical presence and local credibility in Malaysia. This plan does not assume that problem is solved — it names the options for the founder to choose:
1. A Malaysia-based co-founder or partner who takes the lead on in-country relationships.
2. A paid local advisor/fixer relationship for the specific task of RL introductions and ministry navigation.
3. Accept a slower timeline built around founder travel constraints, and budget trips explicitly (§6.5's travel line item) rather than assuming remote outreach substitutes for presence.

No default is asserted here — this is a real decision the founder needs to make, not one automation or this document can resolve.

## 16. What This Plan Does Not Solve

It cannot obtain a BMET Recruiting Licence, an umbrella agreement, or ministry confirmation — those require real relationships and travel, not automation. It cannot make worker-pays viable — that's closed, not a research question. It cannot deploy a single worker until the corridor legally reopens and §2.3/§2.4 resolve. It cannot guarantee ministries grant access even via the RL-mediated path — that's a real risk this plan manages by sequencing and honest scenario-bounding, not one it eliminates. And it cannot decide the Malaysia-presence question (§13) for the founder — that decision belongs to a human, not a document.
