**Bangladesh → Malaysia Labour Corridor: Compliance & Processing Venture**

**Version:** 2.0 (Round 1 hardened) | **Date:** 2026-07-19 | **Status:** Pre-launch, corridor closed, actively preparing
**Hardened against:** Independent AI-generated critique ("Bypassing the Agents," 274 citations) — see `hardening_BUSINESS_PLAN/rounds/r1_investor_review.md` for the full objection log this revision responds to.

> Changes from v1.0 are substantial, not cosmetic: the pricing model is rebuilt around a hard affordability constraint, a Day-1 gating task now sits above all outreach activity, a real MVP and pilot are defined, and two previously-unaddressed risk categories (rising employer-side costs, payment/remittance compliance) are now modeled.

## 1. Executive Summary

The Malaysia-Bangladesh labour corridor has been closed to new recruitment since June 1, 2024. It is reopening — Bangladesh's PM visited Kuala Lumpur in June 2026 to request it, both governments agreed to an Employer Pays Principle in April 2026, and Malaysia centralized quota processing into FWCMS eQuota in July 2026. No labour MoU is signed yet.

**This venture is a Bangladesh-side compliance and processing partner** — not a middleman, not a matchmaker — built to plug into whatever the official system turns out to be (FWCMS, possibly TURAP), handling the functions no Malaysia-based platform can do from Kuala Lumpur: BMET registration, medical, PDOT training, village sourcing, document collection, financing coordination, arrival handling, and bank guarantees.

**The single most important change in this revision:** the worker-pays pricing track is retired as a standalone revenue model. It is not viable at any price a target worker's family can actually pay — this is now treated as an arithmetic fact, not a soft risk (see §6.1). **Revenue is 100% employer-funded.** Worker financing survives only as a bridge mechanism for the rare cases where an employer-pays deal isn't yet in place, never as a pricing pillar.

**The second most important change:** resolving two specific regulatory unknowns — the TURAP fee structure and the BMET processing circular — is now the plan's Day-1 gating task, ranked above every outreach and marketing activity. Every cost projection in this document is conditional on closing that gap (§2.3).

**What's already built, at zero cost:** a bilingual marketing site, a 505-company employer database with sector/compliance scoring, outreach automation, and a 6-channel distribution playbook. **What this revision adds:** a defined MVP (§8), a defined pilot program (§9), a payment/remittance compliance plan (§10), a data-freshness plan for the scraped database (§11), and a rebuilt financial model that survives rising employer-side costs (§6).

### 2.1 Facts as of 2026-07-19

| Fact | Status |
|---|---|
| Corridor closed for new BD recruitment | Since 2024-06-01 |

| FWCMS fee | RM215/worker (confirmed, current) |
| TURAP (Bestinet's proposed platform) | **Fee structure still undocumented.** Not Cabinet-approved as of last check. |
| Malaysia's "10 conditions" for agencies | Would qualify only 5-7 of 102+ incumbent agencies |
| Sarawak FWTA fee | **New:** RM1,854/worker, under review after industry pushback |

### 2.2 The affordability reality (why worker-pays is dead as a pricing model)

| Metric | Value | Source basis |
|---|---|---|
| Bangladesh government legal maximum migration cost | Tk 78,990 | Official cap |
| Actual costs currently paid in the exploitative market | Tk 450,000-500,000 | Independent reporting |
| Average migrant family remittance capacity | ~Tk 82,000 | Remittance data |

Read together: the *legal ceiling* on migration cost already exceeds what an average family can remit. A venture proposing to charge workers 3.0-3.5 lakh BDT (300,000-350,000 Tk) — even while calling it "50% below market" — is still charging **3.6-4.3x** what a family can actually pay upfront. This is not a pricing decision, it's exclusion from the venture's own target market. Worker-pays is retired as a pricing model (see §6.1 for the rebuilt financial model).

### 2.3 Day-1 gating task: resolve TURAP and BMET before anything else scales

Two unknowns make every downstream number in this plan conditional:
1. **TURAP fee structure** — not yet documented or Cabinet-confirmed.
2. **BMET processing circular** — detailed procedural guidelines not yet committed.

**Action, starting today, ranked above outreach volume:** schedule direct engagement with Malaysia's Ministry of Human Resources (KESUMA/HRM) and Bangladesh's Bureau of Manpower, Employment and Training (BMET). The explicit goal is written confirmation of (a) the TURAP fee, if and when it applies, and (b) the BMET workflow and timeline for processing. Until this is resolved, treat every cost/timeline figure in this plan as a scenario, not a forecast — and say so explicitly to any employer or partner this venture talks to. Overstating certainty here is a credibility risk worse than the uncertainty itself.

This is not a task automation can do. It requires a named person with a travel budget and calendar time in Dhaka and Kuala Lumpur. **Nothing else in this plan matters if this doesn't happen.**

## 3. Business Model

### 3.1 What we are

A Bangladesh-side compliance and processing partner, positioned to Malaysian employers as "the FWCMS/TURAP-compatible fulfilment arm" and funded entirely by them. To Bangladeshi workers, positioned as "the zero-fee, transparent, direct channel" — not "the cheaper channel," because at any price we'd charge workers directly, we are not actually affordable to them (§2.2).

### 3.2 The eight functions only a BD-side partner can perform

1. BMET registration & smart card
2. Pre-departure medical (BMET-approved centres)
3. PDOT (Pre-Departure Orientation Training)
4. Village-level worker sourcing
5. Document collection (passport, police clearance, certificates)
6. Worker financing / microfinance coordination — **bridge mechanism only, see §6.1**
7. Arrival handling (KLIA pickup, transit, transport)
8. Bank guarantee processing

### 3.3 Legal structure — start without a licence, grow into one

Unchanged from prior analysis, still the correct sequencing:
1. **Now:** information & registration service only — no recruiting, no money collection.
2. **Soon:** umbrella agreement with an existing BMET Recruiting Licence (RL) holder — 30-50% revenue share.
3. **Medium-term:** Malaysia entity (SSM company + KDN agency registration) + Bangladesh sourcing arm.
4. **At scale:** own BMET RL.

**Action this quarter, unchanged and still the single highest-leverage non-technical task:** start conversations with 2-3 respected, mid-sized BMET RL holders about an umbrella deal. Run this in parallel with §2.3's ministry engagement — they are the two highest-priority relationship-building tasks, and neither is automatable.

### 3.4 Pricing — rebuilt around a single funding source

**There is no worker-pays pricing track.** Revenue is 100% employer-funded, structured as a per-worker processing/compliance fee, sized to what the total employer-side cost structure can actually absorb (see §6 for the full sensitivity model against rising government fees like FWTA).

Worker financing (BRAC/ASA/Grameen) exists only as a bridge: in scenarios where an employer-pays contract isn't yet finalized for a specific batch, a worker *may* access microfinance to cover the legal-maximum Tk 78,990 fee — never the inflated market rate, and never framed as this venture's own pricing. If we cannot find an employer-pays arrangement for a given batch, we do not process that batch on a worker-pays basis; we wait or decline. This is a hard rule, not a fallback preference.

### 3.5 Differentiation — documentation and regulatory-relationship depth, not price

Incumbents can and will temporarily undercut price — they have room to. Our moat is:
- Actual written regulatory confirmation from HRM and BMET that competitors haven't bothered to obtain (§2.3)
- Full BMET/FOMEMA/FWCMS documentation chain, auditable
- Zero worker-paid fees, structurally, not as a marketing claim
- A working MVP (§8) that demonstrates compliance capability before we've moved a single worker

## 6. Financial Model (rebuilt)

### 6.1 Why worker-pays is retired, in numbers

| Price point | Tk amount | vs. legal max (78,990) | vs. avg. remittance capacity (~82,000) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Prior plan's "launch price" | 300,000-350,000 | 3.8-4.4x over | 3.7-4.3x over |
| Prior plan's "at-scale price" | 250,000 | 3.2x over | 3.0x over |
| Current market (exploitative) rate | 450,000-500,000 | 5.7-6.3x over | 5.5-6.1x over |

At every price point this venture previously considered charging workers, the amount exceeds what an average family can remit, before even reaching the legal cap. There is no worker-pays price low enough to be both (a) affordable and (b) commercially sustainable given the cost floor below. **Conclusion: don't compete on worker price at all — compete on being the only channel that doesn't charge the worker.**

### 6.2 Cost floor per worker (Malaysia-side, unchanged sourcing)

| Component | Cost |
|---|---|
| Levy (mfg/construction/services) | RM1,850/yr (RM640/yr plantation/agri) |
| FWCMS processing | RM215 |
| VDR visa fee | ~RM250 |
| FOMEMA medical | ~RM180-250 |
| Insurance | ~RM500/yr |
| Airfare | ~RM1,000-1,200 |
| Security bond (refundable) | ~RM500 |
| **Subtotal (prior baseline)** | **~RM4,200-4,750** |
| **Sarawak FWTA fee (new, region-specific)** | **+RM1,854** |
| **Revised subtotal where FWTA applies** | **~RM6,054-6,604** |

### 6.3 Sensitivity: rising total employer-side cost

Industry reporting suggests total employer-side recruitment cost could rise from a ~RM5,900/worker baseline toward **RM23,600-29,500/worker** (a 4-5x increase) as new fees like FWTA generalize. This directly constrains how much processing fee an employer can absorb on top. Three scenarios:

| Scenario | Total employer-side gov't cost/worker | Room for our processing fee | Implication |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Base** (current, FWTA regional only) | ~RM6,000-6,600 | Meaningful — employers still save vs. incumbent opacity/inefficiency | Proceed as planned |
| **Moderate rise** (FWTA-like fees spread to more states/sectors) | ~RM12,000-15,000 | Compressed — fee must be justified purely on efficiency/compliance value, not cost savings | Reposition pitch: "we save you time and compliance risk," not "we're cheaper" |
| **Severe rise** (4-5x scenario materializes broadly) | ~RM24,000-29,500 | Minimal — employers may resist any added fee | Fee must be bundled into demonstrable ROI (avoided penalties, faster approval) or absorbed as a smaller flat SaaS-style charge, not per-worker |

**This sensitivity table did not exist in the prior version of this plan and must be revisited monthly against `reports/QUOTA_WATCH.md`.**

### 6.4 Revenue model

Per-worker or flat compliance/processing fee charged to the employer, sized against whichever scenario in §6.3 is currently live. No fixed number is asserted here because the honest answer, per §2.3, is that it's conditional on regulatory confirmation still outstanding.

### 6.5 Burn rate

Unchanged: target $0 pre-launch. All tooling free-tier. Only planned spend remains ~$5 for printed QR flyers, plus real travel cost for the §2.3 and §3.3 relationship-building tasks — the first non-zero, necessary spend this plan should budget for.

## 8. The MVP (new)

Before any worker is processed, build a narrow, demonstrable product: **an employer-facing dashboard that tracks the status of their FWCMS eQuota application and lets them securely submit documents for a candidate.** This is deliberately small:

- Solves a real, immediate employer pain point (FWCMS status visibility) independent of whether TURAP/worker-pays questions are resolved
- Requires no BMET licence, no worker deployment capability, no payment handling
- Generates the first real employer relationships and trust before this venture has legal standing to do more
- Serves as the conversion destination for the outreach machine already built (§5) — outreach without this was lead-gen with nowhere to send the lead

**This did not exist in the prior version of this plan.** Building it is now a parallel-track priority alongside §2.3 and §3.3.

---

## 9. The Pilot (new)

A defined, named validation step between "outreach" and "revenue":

- **Cohort:** 5-10 trustworthy SMEs in Malaysia, paired with a similar number of pre-vetted Bangladeshi workers
- **Purpose:** run the full operational workflow end-to-end at small scale, surface bottlenecks, and generate the case studies §4.2 already says are required before approaching larger employers
- **Gate:** the pilot does not start until §2.3 (regulatory confirmation) has at least a preliminary answer — running a pilot on entirely unconfirmed fee/process assumptions risks building trust on a foundation that changes under the pilot's feet

---

## 10. Payment and Remittance Compliance (new)

Any money flow this venture touches — employer fee collection, worker financing disbursement, or anything resembling escrow — is a regulated activity in Bangladesh. Bangladesh Bank's Foreign Exchange Policy Department (FEPD) circulars govern outward remittances and require transactions through Authorized Dealers (ADs). Before this venture handles a single payment:

- Determine whether the chosen legal structure (§3.3) requires its own AD relationship or can route through a partner bank/RL holder's existing arrangement
- Never hold worker or employer funds in an informal account structure
- Treat this as a blocker to resolve alongside §2.3, not an afterthought once revenue starts

---

## 11. Data Freshness Plan (new)

The 505-company database is scraped from public sources that change without notice. This plan now commits to:
- Re-running `scrapers/contact_enricher.py` on a defined cadence (monthly minimum, already wired into the weekly GH Actions job — verify it's actually catching stale entries, not just adding new ones)
- Treating any outreach bounce/dead-contact signal as a trigger to flag that row for re-verification, not silent deletion
- Budgeting maintenance time for this as an ongoing cost, not a one-time build

## 12. What This Plan Does Not Solve

Unchanged in spirit, sharpened by this round:
- It cannot get a BMET Recruiting Licence, an umbrella agreement, or ministry confirmation — those require real relationships and travel, not automation.
- It cannot make the worker-pays model viable — that's now treated as closed, not a research question.
- It cannot deploy a single worker until the corridor legally reopens **and** §2.3 is resolved.
