## 1. Executive Summary

Unchanged core thesis from Round 1: this venture is a Bangladesh-side compliance and processing partner, 100% employer-funded (worker-pays is retired, see §6.1), built to survive the corridor's continued closure and plug into whatever official digital system (FWCMS, NIISe, or TURAP) is live when it reopens.

**What Round 2 changes:** the MVP (§8) no longer depends on unconfirmed third-party access to FWCMS — it's redesigned around a capability this venture can deliver regardless of which government platform is authoritative. A data protection compliance program now exists on paper before it's needed in practice (§10). The financial model now has bounded, scenario-based illustrative numbers an investor can actually underwrite (§6.4). And the plan now states plainly that regulatory access likely *depends on* the RL partnership, not runs alongside it — which changes the sequencing of the first 90 days (§2.4, §13).

## 2. Market Situation and the Day-1 Gating Task

### 2.1-2.2 (unchanged from Round 1 — see r1_hardened_plan.md for full detail)

Corridor closed since 2024-06-01, no labour MoU signed, Employer Pays Principle bilaterally agreed, FWCMS eQuota centralized July 2026. Worker-pays retired as a pricing model: the Bangladesh legal maximum migration cost (Tk 78,990) already exceeds average family remittance capacity (~Tk 82,000 total, not per-fee), and actual market costs (Tk 450,000-500,000) are 5.5-6x that.

### 2.3 The regulatory unknowns

Two unknowns remain unresolved and gate every downstream number: the TURAP fee structure and the BMET processing circular.

### 2.4 Corrected sequencing: regulatory access likely depends on the RL partnership — plan accordingly (new)

Round 1 treated ministry engagement (§2.3 activity) and umbrella RL negotiation (§3.3) as independent parallel tracks. That's optimistic. Malaysia's 10-condition regime and Bangladesh's entrenched incumbent-agency network make it plausible that KESUMA/BMET give little access to an unlicensed, pre-revenue outside party — and that credible regulatory access requires arriving through, or alongside, an established RL holder who already has standing with these ministries.

**Revised sequencing:**
1. **First:** pursue the umbrella RL relationship (§3.3) as the lead workstream, not a parallel one. Evaluate 2-3 candidate RL holders explicitly on *how much ministry access and credibility they bring*, not only on revenue-share terms.
2. **Alongside, not ahead of it:** attempt direct ministry outreach independently — but treat any response (or non-response) as a data point on whether the "parallel tracks" assumption holds, and be ready to fall back fully to the RL-mediated path if direct outreach goes nowhere within, say, 4-6 weeks.
3. Do not commit MVP or pilot resources against a specific TURAP/BMET assumption until one of these two paths produces a real answer.

## 8. The MVP (redesigned)

**Round 1's concept — a dashboard that tracks FWCMS eQuota status via integration — is retired.** No confirmed third-party API exists, and FWCMS itself is being phased out by NIISe (three-phase rollout, March 2026-Oct 2028, 29.9% complete as of the Home Ministry's own June 2026 report). Building against it now risks rework before validation is even complete.

**Redesigned MVP: a platform-agnostic compliance-readiness tool.** Concretely:
- A guided checklist/document-readiness tool that walks an employer through exactly what FWCMS (or, later, NIISe) requires for a Section 60K/eQuota application — quota justification documentation, worker document checklists, levy/insurance proof — with status tracked in our own system, not by scraping or integrating with a government platform.
- Value to the employer: clarity and hand-holding through a genuinely confusing bureaucratic process, independent of which government system is authoritative this month.
- If and when §2.3/§2.4 produces confirmed information about official third-party integration paths (for FWCMS, NIISe, or TURAP), integration becomes an enhancement, not a dependency the MVP's core value rests on.

This is a smaller, less impressive-sounding MVP than Round 1's version — that's the point. It's buildable and durable rather than impressive and fragile.

## 10. Data Protection & PDPA Compliance Program (new)

Malaysia's 2024 PDPA amendments extend the Security Principle directly to data processors — this venture takes on independent legal liability the moment it handles employer FWCMS-application data or worker documents (passport, medical, police clearance), which both the redesigned MVP (§8) and the existing outreach CRM already do or will do. Before either goes further:

- Name a single person (even solo-founder, initially) as responsible for data protection — not a committee, a named accountable individual.
- Minimum data retention: collect only what the compliance-readiness checklist (§8) actually needs; do not retain full copies of sensitive documents (passports, medical results) longer than the active transaction requires.
- Data processing agreements: every employer client relationship gets a written data handling agreement, even at pilot scale.
- Breach protocol: a written (even if simple) incident response plan before the MVP has its first real user, not after an incident.
- Revisit this section once §2.4 produces clarity on which government platform's data-sharing terms actually apply.

## 12. Team & Capacity Plan (new)

Round 2 review correctly flagged that this plan now asks for four simultaneous demanding workstreams — regulatory/RL relationship-building (§2.4/§3.3), MVP build (§8), pilot management (§9), and ongoing outreach (§5) — without saying who does what. Addressed honestly:

- **If this is a solo-founder venture today:** sequence, don't parallelize. §2.4's revised sequencing (RL relationship first) is also a capacity decision — one person cannot credibly run two-country ministry relationships and a product build at the same time. The RL relationship-building phase should be the near-exclusive focus of the founder's time for the first 60-90 days; MVP development should be scoped small enough (§8's redesign helps here) to either be built solo in short, disconciously-scheduled bursts, or contracted out.
- **If there is any ability to bring in a co-founder or early hire:** the highest-leverage addition is not a developer, it's someone with existing Bangladesh recruitment-industry or Malaysia HR/compliance-consulting relationships — i.e., someone who shortens §2.4/§3.3, which is the plan's actual critical path.
- **What not to do:** don't let outreach volume (§5) scale ahead of the founder's actual capacity to have real regulatory/RL conversations. A large lead pipeline sitting unconverted while the founder is in Dhaka meetings is a fine outcome; a founder stretched across five workstreams with none of them actually moving is not.
