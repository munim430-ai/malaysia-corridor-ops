## Objection 1 — The worker-pays track is not "thin margin," it's arithmetically impossible for the target demographic

**Probability of Rejection if Unresolved: 90%**

`BUSINESS_PLAN.md` §3.4 and §6.2 still lead with a worker-pays track (3.0-3.5 lakh BDT launch price) as a live pricing option, softened only by a note that microfinance partnerships would help affordability. The evidence says this isn't a soft constraint:

- The average migrant family's remittance capacity is **~Tk 82,000**, not the 3.0-3.5 lakh BDT this plan proposes charging upfront.
- The Bangladesh government's own **legal maximum** migration cost is Tk 78,990 — and even *that* legal ceiling exceeds typical remittance capacity.
- Actual costs paid in the current (exploitative) market run **Tk 450,000-500,000** — confirming the affordability gap is structural, not a temporary market distortion this venture can undercut its way past.

A plan that still presents worker-pays as a primary track, with MFI partnership as a nice-to-have, will not survive investor diligence. The plan needs to state plainly: **worker-pays is not viable as a standalone revenue model at any price point workers can actually afford**, full stop — and that the entire model must be employer-funded, with MFI/worker financing recast as a bridge mechanism at most, not a pricing pillar.

## Objection 2 — The single highest-risk gap in the plan is treated as a background risk-register line, not the #1 gating task

**Probability of Rejection if Unresolved: 85%**

`BLOCKERS.md` (already in this repo) lists "Undocumented TURAP Fees" and "Uncommitted BMET Circulars" as active blockers. `BUSINESS_PLAN.md` acknowledges TURAP uncertainty in its risk register (§7) but does not make resolving it the first, gating action before any further build-out. Without official, written confirmation from Malaysia's Ministry of Human Resources (KESUMA/HRM) on the TURAP fee structure, and from Bangladesh's BMET on processing circulars, **every cost projection, timeline, and margin calculation in this plan is speculative.** An investor will ask: "What have you done, concretely, to get these two numbers?" and today the honest answer is nothing beyond monitoring public news. This must become the plan's Day-1 action, ranked above outreach volume targets.

## Objection 3 — The financial model doesn't account for the newest, most direct threat to margin: rising *employer-side* mandatory costs

**Probability of Rejection if Unresolved: 70%**

Since this plan's cost model was last updated, Sarawak introduced a **Foreign Workers Transformation Approach (FWTA) fee of RM1,854/worker**, and industry reporting suggests total employer-side recruitment cost could rise **4-5x from a previous RM5,900/worker baseline — toward RM23,600-29,500/worker**. This directly threatens the employer-pays track's viability: the plan assumes employers have budget headroom to absorb a processing fee on top of existing costs, but if government-mandated fees alone are quintupling, that headroom may not exist. The plan needs a sensitivity case: at what total employer-side cost does our processing fee become the item that gets cut first?

## Objection 4 — No MVP. The plan jumps straight to a full marketing/outreach machine before validating the one thing employers actually need

**Probability of Rejection if Unresolved: 60%**

This repo has already built a bilingual site, a 505-company database, and outreach automation — real assets, but all pointed at *lead generation*. Nothing in `BUSINESS_PLAN.md` defines a minimum viable *product*: something an employer can actually use to solve their FWCMS-navigation pain, before the venture has any legal standing to process a worker. An investor will note that lead-gen infrastructure without a product to convert those leads into is marketing spend looking for a use case. The plan should specify a narrow, buildable MVP — e.g., a dashboard that tracks an employer's FWCMS eQuota application status and lets them securely submit documents for a candidate — that can exist and generate trust *before* full worker deployment capability.

## Objection 5 — No defined pilot. The plan goes from 30-day marketing targets directly to revenue projections with nothing in between

**Probability of Rejection if Unresolved: 55%**

There's no cohort-based validation step. A credible plan names a specific pilot: e.g., 5-10 trustworthy SMEs paired with a small number of pre-vetted workers, run end-to-end, generating the case studies and testimonials the plan's own §4.2 says are needed before approaching larger employers. Without this, "SME-first" is a targeting philosophy, not an execution plan.

## Objection 6 — Payment and remittance compliance infrastructure is entirely unaddressed

**Probability of Rejection if Unresolved: 50%**

Bangladesh Bank's Foreign Exchange Policy Department (FEPD) circulars govern outward remittances strictly, requiring transactions through Authorized Dealers. If this venture ever holds funds in escrow, or facilitates any BD-to-MY or MY-to-BD payment flow, that's a regulated activity with its own licensing and compliance burden the plan doesn't mention at all. An investor will ask "how does money actually move" and the plan currently has no answer.

## Objection 7 — Scraped-data dependency is a fragile foundation, and the plan doesn't say how it stays accurate

**Probability of Rejection if Unresolved: 40%**

The 505-company database is a real asset, but it depends on scraping public sources that change, rebrand, or restructure without notice. There's no monitoring/refresh SLA, no data-decay assumption, and no plan for what happens when a scraped contact goes stale mid-outreach. This is a lower-severity objection than 1-3, but a diligence-minded investor will flag it as a maintenance cost the model doesn't currently budget for.
