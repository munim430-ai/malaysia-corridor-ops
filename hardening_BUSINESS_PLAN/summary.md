## Strategic Evolution

**Round 0 → Round 1:** The original plan treated worker-pays as a live, if thin-margin, pricing track and treated the TURAP/BMET regulatory unknowns as background risks. An independent critique (274 sourced citations) showed the affordability gap is structural — the legal maximum migration fee already exceeds average remittance capacity — and that every financial projection in the plan is conditional on two specific regulatory confirmations that no one has yet obtained. Round 1 responds by making the business 100% employer-funded, promoting regulatory engagement to the top of the action list, and adding four previously-missing structural elements (MVP, pilot, payment compliance, data freshness). Token footprint: original plan 6,129 tokens (gpt2) → Round 1 hardened plan 4,615 tokens (gpt2) — more disciplined, not just longer.

**Round 1 → Round 2:** Fresh web research surfaced that Round 1's proposed MVP (an FWCMS status-tracking dashboard) had no confirmed path to third-party platform access, and that FWCMS itself is mid-replacement by Malaysia's NIISe system (29.9% complete as of June 2026, full rollout by October 2028) — meaning the MVP as conceived would be built on a moving, unconfirmed target. Round 2 also surfaced that Malaysia's 2024 PDPA amendments create direct legal liability for this venture as a data processor, that the plan lacked any number an investor could underwrite, and that the plan's two "parallel" Day-1 priorities (ministry engagement and RL negotiation) are more likely sequential — RL credibility probably unlocks regulatory access, not the reverse. Round 2 responds with a smaller, platform-agnostic MVP, a data protection program, a bounded illustrative revenue model, and a corrected roadmap sequencing. Token footprint: Round 1 hardened plan 4,615 tokens (gpt2) → Round 2 hardened plan 3,506 tokens (gpt2) — each round has gotten more precise, not longer.

**Round 2 → Round 3:** A redline audit of Round 2 against Round 1 caught a real defect: the Payment & Remittance Compliance section from Round 1 had been silently overwritten rather than renumbered when Round 2 added the Data Protection section — restored as its own section in the final plan. Fresh web research also confirmed KESUMA's foreign-worker-system review runs through "preliminary engagement sessions with labour source countries and industry stakeholders, including the technology provider [Bestinet]" — meaning Round 2's assumption that independent ministry outreach was a real parallel path to RL-mediated access is optimistic; the final plan downgrades it to opportunistic-only. Round 3 also closed three long-standing gaps that had survived all three rounds unaddressed: no primary market validation, no reconciliation of illustrative revenue against real costs, and no answer to who is physically present in Malaysia to do relationship-building. Token footprint across the full loop: original plan 6,129 tokens (gpt2) → Round 1 hardened 4,615 → Round 2 hardened 3,506 → final (Round 3) hardened 6,108 — the final round grew again because it restored a dropped section and added net-new structural content (§4.3, §6.5, §13), not because it re-added padding; each round's investor-review-to-hardened-plan ratio stayed disciplined (investor reviews ran 1,190-1,732 tokens each, a fraction of the plan itself).
