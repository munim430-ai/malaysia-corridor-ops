# Sample Demand Letters / Job Orders: Furniture, Rubber/Glove, Security (Malaysia-Bangladesh corridor)

Target: real, historical demand letters / job orders / manpower requisition letters from Malaysian employers
in **furniture manufacturing**, **rubber/glove manufacturing**, and **security services**, dated before
June 2024. Individual worker personal-data documents (passport/NID-bearing) were excluded by design.

Overall result: no fully-visible, named-employer demand letter could be confirmed for all three target
sectors. What *was* found and verified: a genuine, currently-live set of blank demand-letter / job-order
templates published by a real, operating Malaysian recruitment agency, which explicitly cover the
furniture and rubber/glove sub-sectors (as part of "Manufacturing") and clarify that the "Services" sector
category used for these letters does **not** include security guards. One additional real named-employer
demand letter was found in preview form on Scribd but could not be confirmed as furniture/rubber/security
(see "Al-Rabeta International" below) — sector is most likely general labour/construction, flagged as
uncertain rather than presented as a match.

## Agensi Pekerjaan Exelite (M) Sdn. Bhd. — "SERVICES SECTOR" eligibility sheet (negative finding for Security)

- **Source URL:** `https://exelite.com.my/wp-content/uploads/form/service/SERVICES.doc`
- **Sector:** Services — listed sub-categories are Restaurant, Cleaning, Laundry, Cargo Handling, Resort
  Island, Welfare Homes, Grocery, Wholesale, Textile, Barber, and Scrap/Recycled metals. **Security guard
  work is notably absent from this list.**
- **Why this matters:** cross-checked against news/policy sources (CESLAM, IOM, FMT reporting) — under
  Malaysia's Ministry of Home Affairs (MOHA) policy, **Nepal has an exclusive monopoly on supplying
  foreign security guards to Malaysia**; Bangladesh is not an approved source country for the security
  guard occupation. This is the most likely explanation for why no Bangladesh-to-Malaysia security-sector
  demand letter could be located anywhere in this search — such a document may not exist because the
  recruitment channel itself does not exist for that nationality/sector combination.
- **Representative excerpt (verbatim):**

  > SERVICES SECTOR
  > Restaurant — No. of Seats / Helper: 70-140, 140-250
  > Cleaning — Monthly Contract value / RM 2500.00 = Total number of workers
  > Laundry — 3 Machines per 1 worker
  > Cargo Handling — Monthly Sales Value / 2500 ... Having a Letter of Confirmation and Support Letter
  > from the Local Port/Airport Authority
  > ...
  > Scrap/Recycled metals/Goods — Processing Rate in weight (Tons) of materials per day: 5-10 Tons: 3
  > workers; 10-50 Tons: 5 workers; >50 Tons: 15 workers. Having a business License with the Local
  > Authority. Licensed issued by the Royal Malaysian Police.

## Search process

Queries tried (10+), across general web search, Scribd, PDFCoffee, Docplayer, Academia.edu, SlideShare,
and Bengali-language search:

1. `site:scribd.com "demand letter" Malaysia furniture worker Bangladesh`
2. `site:scribd.com "job order" Malaysia glove factory worker`
3. `"manpower requisition" Malaysia rubber OR furniture Bangladesh BMET`
4. `"demand letter for recruitment of workers" Malaysia security guard`
5. `site:docplayer.net demand letter Malaysia security worker Bangladesh`
6. `site:pdfcoffee.com demand letter Malaysia workers Bangladesh`
7. `"demand letter" furniture factory Malaysia Bangladesh workers BMET`
8. `"job order" security guard company Malaysia Bangladesh recruitment agency demand`
9. `চাহিদাপত্র মালয়েশিয়া গ্লাভস শ্রমিক` (Bengali: "demand letter Malaysia gloves worker")
10. `চাহিদাপত্র মালয়েশিয়া ফার্নিচার শ্রমিক নিয়োগ` (Bengali: "demand letter Malaysia furniture worker recruitment")
11. `site:academia.edu OR site:slideshare.net demand letter Malaysia rubber glove worker`
12. `site:scribd.com "Sdn Bhd" "demand letter" furniture` / `security services` / `rubber glove`
13. `"job order" "Sdn Bhd" furniture Bangladesh worker recruitment agency`
14. `"Riverstone" OR "Careplus" OR "YTY" glove Sdn Bhd demand letter foreign worker Bangladesh recruit`
15. `"security services" Sdn Bhd Malaysia "demand letter" foreign worker guard recruitment license`
16. `"furniture" Malaysia "manpower request" OR "quota approval" Bangladesh worker recruiting agency letter`
17. `Malaysia private security guard foreign worker source country policy Nepal only Bangladesh not allowed`

**What worked:**
- Direct web search surfaced several genuine Scribd-hosted demand letters (Al-Rabeta International,
  System Care Sdn Bhd, Exelite Resources) and, crucially, surfaced a Malaysian recruitment agency's own
  public downloads page (exelite.com.my), which hosts real, currently-live `.doc` templates organized by
  sector (agriculture / construction / manufacturing / plantation / service). These downloaded cleanly (no
  login) and were read via `strings` extraction from the binary — yielding verifiable, sector-specific
  demand-letter and eligibility-criteria content, including explicit mentions of the furniture and
  rubber/glove sub-sectors.
- Bengali-language search surfaced a Bangla Tribune news article confirming Malaysia's glove industry
  (via MARGMA, the Malaysian Rubber Glove Manufacturers Association) formally petitioned the government
  for foreign workers, corroborating that glove-sector demand exists, though the article itself is news
  coverage, not a primary demand letter document.

**What didn't work:**
- Scribd's individual document pages (`scribd.com/document/...`) are gated behind a JavaScript
  "Client Challenge" bot-detection wall that blocked WebFetch and direct `curl` (both returned only the
  challenge shell, not content). A read-only rendering proxy (r.jina.ai) was tried on one URL as a way to
  see the same freely-visible preview a logged-out browser would show; it surfaced only Scribd's AI-written
  one-paragraph description snippet plus a "Download to read ad-free" prompt for the actual page images —
  it did not reveal additional body text, so no further attempt was made to extract text from the gated
  page images (this would have required OCR-ing paywalled preview images, which was treated as
  off-limits per instructions).
- PDFCoffee and Academia.edu direct document fetches returned HTTP 403.
- Docplayer searches returned no on-topic results (general Malaysia/Bangladesh business documents only).
- No search (English or Bengali) surfaced a fully-readable, named-Malaysian-employer demand letter
  specifically for furniture manufacturing or rubber/glove manufacturing with complete visible body text.
- No search surfaced any Bangladesh-sourced security-guard demand letter at all — cross-referencing this
  against labour-migration policy sources indicates this is very likely a structural absence (Bangladesh
  is not an approved source country for Malaysia's security guard occupation; that channel is reserved for
  Nepal), not a gap in search effort.

## Not found

- **Furniture manufacturing:** No specific, named-employer demand letter with fully visible content was
  found. The Exelite "Manufacturing" template/eligibility-sheet pair is the closest genuine artifact
  (explicitly lists "Furniture industry" as a covered sub-sector) but is a blank template, not a completed
  letter naming a real furniture company.
- **Rubber/glove manufacturing:** Same situation as furniture — the Exelite Manufacturing template
  explicitly names "Rubber-based industry" and "Medical related products and Glove industry," and a Bangla
  news article confirms MARGMA (glove manufacturers' association) petitioned for foreign workers, but no
  completed, named-employer demand letter with visible salary/quota specifics for a glove/rubber factory
  was located.
- **Security services:** No Bangladesh-to-Malaysia security-guard demand letter was found. Based on
  cross-referenced policy sources, this sector is very likely structurally closed to Bangladeshi workers
  (Nepal holds an effective monopoly on the security guard occupation under Malaysian Ministry of Home
  Affairs policy), which would explain the absence rather than indicating incomplete search effort.
