TURAP Malaysia 2026: Complete Guide to the Proposed Recruitment Platform

Last updated July 2026 | 8 min read

What is TURAP?

TURAP (or TURAP Malaysia) is a proposed digital recruitment platform by Bestinet Sdn Bhd — the same company behind the existing FWCMS (Foreign Worker Centralised Management System). If approved, it would become the single mandatory platform for all Bangladesh-to-Malaysia worker recruitment.

Think of it as a government-mandated matchmaking system: Malaysian employers post quota/worker requirements, Bangladesh recruiting agencies upload worker profiles, and the platform facilitates the matching, documentation, and approval process — all in one digital workflow.

⚠️ KEY FACT: TURAP has NOT been approved by the Malaysian Cabinet. Despite reports suggesting otherwise, HR Minister Steven Sim has publicly refuted that TURAP was adopted. It remains a proposal under consideration — not policy. Do not confuse proposal for fact.

Proposed Fee Structure

The fee structure proposed by Bestinet has been the most controversial aspect:

Fee ComponentProposed AmountWho Pays
Platform feeUS$1,000/workerEmployer (proposed)
Additional service fee1 month salary/workerEmployer (proposed)
FWCMS processingRM 215/workerEmployer (existing)
Levy (manufacturing)RM 1,850/yrEmployer (existing)

At US$1,000 (~RM 4,700) plus one month's salary (~RM 1,500), the total platform cost per worker could reach RM 6,200 — on top of existing government fees and processing costs. For a company hiring 500 workers, that's over RM 3.1 million in platform fees alone.

Current Status (July 2026)

  • Status: Under consideration — NOT approved
  • Government position: HR Ministry has refuted adoption reports
  • Employers: Major manufacturing associations (FMM, MEF) have expressed strong reservations about cost
  • Workers: Bangladesh diaspora organizations have raised concerns about cost passed to workers
  • Timeline: Decision linked to corridor reopening MoU negotiations

How TURAP Would Work (If Approved)

  1. Employer Registration: Company registers on TURAP, uploads Section 60K approval / quota allocation
  2. Worker Profiling: Bangladesh agency uploads worker details: bio, passport, skills, medical history
  3. Matching: System matches worker profiles to employer requirements
  4. Document Processing: BMET clearance, FOMEMA scheduling, VDR application via platform
  5. Deployment: Worker travels, undergoes FOMEMA, PLKS issued, employment begins

Arguments For and Against

Arguments FOR TURAP

  • Transparency: End-to-end digital trail reduces document fraud and middleman manipulation
  • Efficiency: Single platform eliminates paperwork delays across multiple agencies
  • Integration: Works with existing FWCMS eQuota, not a replacement
  • Data: Comprehensive worker database for government policy decisions

Arguments AGAINST TURAP

  • Cost: US$1,000/worker + 1-month-salary is extraordinarily high for a platform fee
  • Monopoly: Bestinet would control the entire recruitment pipeline — no competition
  • Existing systems: FWCMS eQuota already handles quota; why duplicate?
  • Worker impact: Despite "employer pays" label, cost ultimately affects wages/contract terms
  • Timing: Adding new platform costs when corridor is already expensive seems counterproductive

What TURAP Means for Bangladesh Workers

If TURAP is approved with a zero-fee-to-worker model (as currently proposed), the direct impact on workers would be minimal — except that employers may offset costs through lower wages or stricter contract terms. The real risk is that if TURAP falls through or is delayed, it could further delay corridor reopening while the system is debated.

What TURAP Means for Malaysian Employers

The biggest concern is cost per worker. At RM 6,200+ in platform fees alone, TURAP would significantly increase hiring costs. For labour-intensive industries (plantations, gloves, construction), this could make Bangladesh workers less competitive compared to other source countries (Nepal, Myanmar, Indonesia) where platform costs may be lower or non-existent.
